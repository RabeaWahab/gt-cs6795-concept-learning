How the three models actually learn

Same task, same feedback, same 8 cards — three completely different ways of making sense of them. Press Step below and watch each model's mind change, one card at a time.

A companion to the results site — this one is about the mechanism, not the score.

The material

Eight cards, three features

Every card differs on just three yes/no features — its size, its shape, and its shade. Three switches, two settings each, gives exactly 8 possible cards. That's the whole deck. A card's identity is a three-digit code like 1·0·1 = large, round, solid.

The job the model is given: sort these 8 cards into two piles of four — pile A and pile B — using only the yes/no answer it gets after each guess. Which four go where is the puzzle, and there are six famous puzzles (Types I–VI), each a different logic. You'll pick one on the bench.

The loop everyone runs

One trial, five beats

All three models sit inside the identical training loop. They differ only in what happens at beat 2 and beat 4 — how they guess, and how they change. Everything else is shared.

BEAT 1

Show a card

One of the 8 cards appears in random order.

BEAT 2

Guess a pile

The model commits to A or B — its own way.

BEAT 3

Hear the truth

"Correct" or "wrong" — the real pile is revealed.

BEAT 4

Adjust

The model nudges its insides — its own way.

BEAT 5

Repeat

All 8 = one block. Count mistakes until a clean block.

The verdict

Only one shape of mind learns like ours

Run every model on every puzzle and count the mistakes. People find them I < II < III ≈ IV ≈ V < VI — and famously, Type II (a two-feature XOR) is easier for people than the single-feature-plus Types III–V. Here's what each model does with the same six puzzles, computed live in your browser:

The one line to remember. The prototype keeps a single average card per pile — so when the two averages collide (Types II and VI) it's blind. The rule seeker wants a tidy logical rule, and since no simple rule cracks Type II, it thrashes — the exact opposite of people. Only the exemplar model, and only with attention turned on, makes Type II easy the way we do: it learns to ignore the useless feature and lock onto the two that matter.

Flip the attention switch off on the bench and re-run Type II — you'll watch that advantage evaporate. That single switch is the whole thesis.
